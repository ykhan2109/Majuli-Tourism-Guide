Majuli Satras: Guardians of Assam's Cultural and Spiritual Heritage



Satras are most significant part of Neo Vaishnavism culture. These are both temple and culture centre for Neo vaishnavite disciple. Most of the Assamese people are followers of Neo Vaishnavism. Saint Srimanata Sankar Deva established 64 satras in Majuli island to spread the culture and education in ancient time. Due to erosion in Majuli island, only 31 satras left. Each satras has its own cultural significance and try to preserve unique artistic attribute in music, literature, dance, drama etc.

The Majuli Satras are the heart of the island's rich cultural and religious heritage, playing a pivotal role in the preservation and promotion of Neo-Vaishnavism, a monotheistic form of Hinduism founded by the Assamese saint Srimanta Sankardeva in the 15th century. These Satras are not merely religious centers; they are vibrant hubs of art, culture, and spirituality.

Historical and Spiritual Significance:



Majuli's Satras were established to propagate the teachings of Srimanta Sankardeva, emphasizing devotion to a single deity, Lord Krishna, through Bhakti (devotion). The Satras serve as monasteries where monks, known as bhakats, lead a life dedicated to spiritual practices, education, and community service. They also play a crucial role in maintaining the cultural fabric of Assam by preserving traditional art forms like music, dance, drama, and mask-making.
